## Profile

- **Name:** Geoff Thomas (Jeff)
- **Pharmacies:** Malpas Pharmacy (since 2003) + Malpas Brook Pharmacy (opened Dec 2019)
- **Location:** Newport, Wales — 0.6 miles apart, shared staff/stock/drivers
- **Volume:** 25–27,000 items/month (Malpas) + 12–13,000 items/month (Malpas Brook)
- **Growth:** From 2,500 items when he opened → 25,000+ now (10x)
- **Previous system:** ProScript / ProScript Connect
- **Titan since:** Dec 2019 (trialled at Malpas Brook, rolled to Malpas in 2020)
- **Came via:** Investec nursing home tech → Tariq pitched PMR development
- **Services expansion:** 3 consultation rooms (2 opening after Easter), 6 ACTs across both sites, IP, aesthetics, earwax, weight management, smoking cessation, contraception

## Recommended Video Cut (90 seconds)

Your v2 edit is too linear — it reads like a transcript, not a story. Here's the restructured cut based on the Prabs formula (hook → problem → shift → payoff → insight → close):

### HOOK (0–8s)
> "Well at Malpas we're doing between 25 to 27,000 items. And at Malpas Brook... we're doing 12 to 13,000 items currently a month."

*Alt hook (stronger if you have the clip):*
> "I went from 2,500 items to over 25,000. I wouldn't cope with that and the old way of doing things."

### THE PROBLEM (8–25s)
> "As you're growing and our items were growing, the demand for the pharmacists to be at the end of everything — you were the funnel, so everything was coming at you, to check. So you had to do the final check and then everything was okay. A lot of pressure to do that. So it was hard work and it was getting harder."

### THE SHIFT (25–45s)
> "What I like about Titan is that you can do your clinical check at the front, and you can release the process, you can release the prescriptions. And because of the barcoding, because of the scanning, you can trust the process. Everything gets scanned, and if it's not right, it'll alert you — that's the wrong product, that's the wrong patient. It connects everything together. And it makes things fast, safe, and efficient."

### THE PAYOFF (45–65s)
> "The fact that you can actually release the prescriptions, clinically check them, and then get everything else done by other members of the team — is freed me up. Get my weekends back."

> "With the funding situation we're in, you've got to be doing services. So you're looking to aesthetics, earwax removal, weight management. All of these services have become quite vital. You want your IP to be released as soon as possible so they can go in the room and actually be helping — building a reputation for running a clinic."

### THE INSIGHT (65–80s)
> "You could kill yourself trying to get a wide range of customers, be overwhelmed and not have time to do any of the services. And all you're doing then is fundamentally dispensing prescriptions we're not making a great deal of money from."

*This is the best soundbite in the interview. The v2 edit misses it entirely.*

### THE CLOSE (80–90s)
> "If another pharmacy owner was thinking about switching, I would invite them to come and see how it works. From my experience, this is another level. This is the way forward. Get on board — because otherwise that ship will sail. You'll be left behind."
