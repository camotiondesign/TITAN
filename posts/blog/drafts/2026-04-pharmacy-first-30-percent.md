# Pharmacy First at 30%: What the Compliance Rate Is Actually Telling Us

**Published:** April 2026 | **Site:** titanpmr.com | **Audience:** Pharmacy owners thinking about service growth

---

Thirty percent.

That's roughly where Pharmacy First sits in terms of full bundle compliance across UK community pharmacy. Three in ten pharmacies delivering everything the service requires. Seven in ten not there yet.

That number is easy to read as a failure story. It's not. It's a design story.

## What the 30% Number Actually Means

Pharmacy First launched as an opportunity. A funded pathway for community pharmacies to consult directly on seven clinical conditions — without the patient needing a GP appointment first. The logic was sound. Pharmacy is accessible. Pharmacists are clinicians. Let them consult.

The uptake problem isn't willingness. It's not clinical competence either. Most pharmacists are more than capable of managing a UTI consultation or assessing a case of sinusitis. The issue is structural. The conditions in the full Pharmacy First bundle don't carry equal weight — and one of them is causing the whole compliance picture to drag.

## What Full Bundle Delivery Actually Requires

Pharmacy First covers seven conditions:

- Acute otitis media (ear infections, for under-18s)
- Sinusitis
- Sore throat
- Infected insect bites
- Impetigo
- Shingles
- Urinary tract infections (UTIs, for women)

Plus, two commissioned services sit alongside the core conditions:

- Contraception (oral emergency and ongoing)
- Hypertension case finding (blood pressure checks with referral pathways)
- And, for full bundle status: **Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitoring (ABPM)**

That last one is where the compliance rate breaks down.

ABPM requires a 24-hour blood pressure monitoring device fitted to the patient, worn overnight, readings analysed, and a clinical summary produced. It is not a quick consultation. It requires a device, a trained clinician, appointment slots, and patient willingness to return. In a high-footfall, walk-in pharmacy, this is genuinely hard to operationalise.

The other conditions are relatively fast. UTI, sinusitis, impetigo — these fit a walk-in consultation model. A patient comes in, you run through a template, you advise or prescribe, they leave. Fifteen minutes, maybe twenty.

ABPM does not work like that. And in a model where every clinical minute has to compete with dispensary demand, the ABPM hurdle is often just too high.

## The Real Barrier: ABPM in a Walk-In Model

ABPM is slow. The device goes on in the morning. The patient wears it for 24 hours. They come back. You download the data. You interpret it. You refer or prescribe.

That's multiple touchpoints, a hardware dependency, and a return visit — in a pharmacy that wasn't designed around booked appointments. Walk-in traffic doesn't naturally convert to 24-hour monitoring cycles.

Low demand compounds the issue. Most patients who could benefit from ABPM aren't presenting to pharmacy asking for it. The referral pathway isn't obvious. GPs refer some patients, but the volume is unpredictable. Proactively identifying candidates in a busy dispensary is hard when you're already managing 600 items a day.

The result: most pharmacies in the 70% are delivering some Pharmacy First conditions well, sometimes very well, and stalling on ABPM specifically. That's not non-compliance across the board. It's one structural obstacle turning a partial service into an incomplete one.

## What the Top 30% Have in Common

The pharmacies consistently delivering the full bundle share three operational characteristics.

**Tracking.** They know where every Pharmacy First consultation sits at any given moment. Not from memory. Not from a spreadsheet someone updates when they remember. From a live workflow dashboard that makes the volume and status of consultations visible in real time. This matters because Pharmacy First is a numbers game: volume drives revenue, and volume requires oversight.

**Templates.** The top-performing pharmacies have standardised consultation templates for every condition in the bundle. Clinical questioning is consistent. Documentation is fast. The pharmacist isn't reinventing the consultation each time — they're running through a structured pathway that produces a compliant clinical record at the end. For ABPM specifically, the template is what makes the process repeatable rather than ad hoc.

**Referral pathways.** These pharmacies have built clear, predictable routes for ABPM candidates — usually from blood pressure opportunistic checking built into other service touchpoints. A patient comes in for a repeat. Blood pressure is checked as a matter of course. If it's elevated, ABPM is offered and booked. Not reactive. Proactive.

These aren't skills advantages. The pharmacists in the 70% are equally qualified. What differs is infrastructure.

## Why PMR Design Determines Compliance More Than Effort

Here is the uncomfortable truth about Pharmacy First compliance: effort isn't the limiting factor.

Pharmacists are already working hard. Dispensing teams are already stretched. Nobody in the 70% is opting out of Pharmacy First for lack of motivation. They're opting out because the system they're running on makes full delivery too hard to sustain alongside the dispensary.

A PMR that wasn't built for clinical service delivery treats Pharmacy First as an add-on. A consultation happens somewhere outside the main workflow. A record gets created in a separate template. Metrics get tracked in a spreadsheet, or not at all. The pharmacist has to mentally toggle between dispensary mode and clinical mode, with no system support for either transition.

A PMR built for clinical services looks different. The Pharmacy First workflow is inside the same environment as the dispensary. Consultation templates are embedded, not external. ABPM tracking — booking, device management, results, referral — is part of the record, not a separate note. Volume and revenue are visible.

The 30% aren't working harder than the 70%. They have better infrastructure. Better infrastructure comes from PMR design decisions made at the system level — not from effort applied at the pharmacy level.

The question isn't whether you want to deliver Pharmacy First in full. The question is whether your system makes that possible.
