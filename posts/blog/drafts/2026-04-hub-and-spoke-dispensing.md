# Hub and Spoke Dispensing: What Independent Pharmacies Need to Know

Hub and spoke has been pharmacy's open secret for years.

The economics make sense. The clinical case is clear. And yet most multi-branch operators have tried to implement it and hit the same wall: their systems weren't designed for it.

That's changing. Here's what you actually need to understand.

## What Hub and Spoke Actually Means

Strip away the policy language and hub and spoke is a simple idea.

One site — the hub — does the dispensing. It handles the volume, the checking, the labelling, the assembly. The other sites — the spokes — handle patient contact, consultations, and clinical services. Finished prescriptions travel from hub to spoke. Patients collect from their local pharmacy.

The goal is efficiency through specialisation. A hub running 400, 500, 600 items a day in a clean, uninterrupted dispensing environment is fundamentally more productive than four different branches each running 100 items a day while simultaneously managing walk-in queries, phone calls, and consultations.

The spokes, freed from the dispensary pressure, become something different. Consultation rooms replace dispensing shelves. Pharmacists see patients instead of checking baskets. The pharmacy looks less like a dispensary and more like a clinic.

That's the model. Simple in principle. Complex in practice.

## Where the Regulation Stands

UK legislation has historically required dispensing to happen at the site where the prescription is presented. Hub and spoke, in its true form, required a regulatory workaround — or a single legal entity operating across multiple branches.

The Human Medicines (Amendment) Regulations 2023 changed this. For the first time, licensed hub and spoke dispensing between separate legal entities became permissible in England. The framework creates a route for any registered pharmacy to act as a hub for another — not just branches within the same ownership group.

The secondary legislation and guidance is still bedding in. NHS England has been working through the commissioning and oversight implications. But the direction is clear: hub and spoke is now a permitted model, and the infrastructure around it is being built.

For multi-branch independents who already own all their sites, the position is simpler. Intra-company hub and spoke has always been possible. What's changed is the system sophistication needed to make it work at any meaningful scale.

## The Operational Challenge Nobody Talks About

The regulatory change gets most of the attention. The harder problem is operational.

Running a hub and spoke model across multiple sites creates a specific set of workflow demands that traditional PMR systems were never designed to handle.

**Stock visibility across sites.** If the hub is dispensing prescriptions on behalf of spokes, it needs to know what stock exists across the whole network — not just its own shelves. When a prescription arrives at spoke level for a patient, the question is: where is that medicine? Is it already at the spoke? Does it need to be sourced from the hub? Is there a stock transfer needed? Without cross-site inventory visibility, this becomes a guessing game. The hub reorders items that already exist somewhere in the network. Spokes run out of items sitting in boxes at the hub.

**Transfer tracking.** When a dispensed prescription moves from hub to spoke, that journey needs to be recorded. Who dispensed it? When? What batch? What expiry? When did it arrive at the spoke? Chain of custody, in regulatory terms. Without a system that tracks this natively, you're doing it on paper — which defeats the efficiency argument.

**Dispensing records across sites.** A patient's dispensing record needs to be coherent regardless of which site they're collecting from or which site dispensed their prescription. Clinical checking, dispensing decisions, any interventions — all of it needs to live in one place, accessible to the right person at the right time, regardless of which branch they're standing in.

This is why hub and spoke breaks down for most operators. The model is sound. The execution infrastructure isn't there.

## Why Most PMR Systems Can't Handle Cross-Site Workflows

A traditional PMR is a single-site system. It was designed to manage the dispensing workflow at one branch. Multiple installations, one per branch, all running independently.

Cross-site visibility wasn't part of the design brief. If you want to see what's happening at branch two from branch one, you're logging into a separate system, or you're picking up the phone.

Stock levels are siloed per branch. Dispensing records don't travel automatically. The hub can't see the spoke's incoming prescription queue. The spoke can't see the hub's dispensing status. Every cross-site interaction is a manual process — someone calling, emailing, or physically moving to communicate information that a connected system would share automatically.

This matters more at scale. Running a two-site operation with a daily phone call between branches is inconvenient. Running a four, five, or six-site operation on disconnected single-site PMRs is a management problem that grows with every branch you add.

Jeet runs Medichem — a four-pharmacy group across Leeds and the surrounding area. Before implementing hub and spoke on Titan PMR, the model looked familiar: branches operating independently, pharmacist staying until 11pm to clear the floor, no visibility between sites.

"I had a young daughter who I don't think I've seen her grow that one year, because by the time I go home, she was off to bed. And by the time I moved out in the morning, she was still asleep."

The operational problem was clear. One branch could be overwhelmed with 1,000 prescriptions and two dispensers. A well-staffed branch 50 miles away had capacity and no way to help. No cross-site workflow meant no cross-site support.

The hub went live on Titan PMR. The numbers that followed are worth reading closely.

Dispenser efficiency at the hub: from 12–15 items per hour to 40–45 items per hour. That's not a marginal improvement. That's a tripling of throughput from the same people, in the same hours, with no additional hiring. The change came from removing interruptions. Hub dispensers dispense. They don't answer the phone, manage walk-in queries, or deal with the operational noise that breaks concentration in a spoke environment.

Stock holding at branch level: reduced by 45%. When the hub manages fulfilment centrally and branches stop needing to maintain large safety stocks for every eventuality, inventory compresses. Less capital tied up in stock sitting on shelves. Fewer expiry write-offs. A leaner operation.

Tote deliveries to each spoke: from 20 per day down to 2. That's the physical expression of the efficiency gain — fewer, fuller, more predictable deliveries to each site.

Jeet now leaves at a normal time. He describes it as: "This was a life changer — as a pharmacist, as a contractor, as a dad, as a husband."

But what Medichem has taken further is where it gets interesting. Beyond traditional hub and spoke — one hub, all spokes route to it — Titan PMR's peer-to-peer functionality allows any branch to send prescriptions to any other branch. Not just to the hub. If the hub is at capacity, branch two can take overflow from branch three directly. The network becomes flexible in ways that a rigid hub-and-spoke topology doesn't allow.

"We've reduced about 45% of stock holding capacity at branch level at the minute by using peer to peer," Jeet says. "Dispensing should be the last thing on everybody's head, and that's what Titan has enabled us to do."
