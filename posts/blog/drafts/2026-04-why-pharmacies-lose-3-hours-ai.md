## Where the Time Actually Goes

Break down a typical dispensary day and the same four tasks appear, over and over.

**Manual prescription tracking.** Without real-time workflow visibility, every team member is constantly asking where things are. Is this script ready? Has this been checked? Where's Mrs Ahmed's dosset box? In the absence of a system that shows workflow status at a glance, the answer is: someone has to go and look. That someone is usually you.

**Phone queries.** A significant portion of dispensary calls are prescription status enquiries — patients and care homes wanting to know if their medication is ready. Without automated notifications, each one requires a staff member to stop, search, and respond. Multiply that across sixty, eighty, a hundred patients a day.

**Stock checks.** Manual stock management means double-handling. Staff check the shelf, check the system, check the system again because the system is wrong. Reorders go in for items already in the building. Items run out because nobody spotted the trend. Every discrepancy costs time.

**Double-entry.** EPS, your PMR, your clinical system, your ordering platform. In most independent pharmacies, these don't talk to each other natively. The result is data being re-entered by hand — a source of error, frustration, and lost minutes that compound into lost hours.

Add these up across a full dispensary day and you're looking at two to three hours of recoverable time. Not theoretical time. Actual clinical and administrative hours that better-designed systems return to the people who should be using them differently.

## Why Traditional PMR Design Makes This Worse

The original PMR was built to label medicines. That was the job. Process a prescription, print a label, move on.

That model hasn't fundamentally changed in most systems — even as the pharmacy's role has. Pharmacy First, weight management clinics, independent prescribing, care home services — these require a different kind of workflow management. Not just labelling, but clinical triage, patient tracking, service scheduling, and intelligent prioritisation.

Traditional PMR design treats all of this as separate. The dispensary workflow is one thing. The clinical record is another. The repeat prescription system is another. The result is a collection of tools that don't speak to each other — and a pharmacist in the middle, manually bridging the gaps.

Every gap costs time. Every manual bridge is a minute that should have been spent with a patient.

## What AI Transcription and Auto-Print Actually Do

Titan PMR approaches this differently. The two features that most directly reclaim dispensary time are AI-assisted clinical checking and automated prescription flow — and they work in combination.

AI clinical checking means the system reads incoming prescriptions, applies clinical logic, flags genuine concerns, and clears the safe ones — without the pharmacist needing to review every item from scratch. The pharmacist sees what needs attention. The AI handles the volume. In practice, this means a pharmacist can manage a dispensary of 15,000 or 20,000 items a month without being chained to a checking bench all day.

Repeat flow automation changes the dosset and repeat prescription process entirely. Instead of a paper diary, manual phone calls to surgeries, and staff physically chasing prescriptions, the system manages the cycle automatically. Prescriptions come in, are tracked, and when they're ready — the patient gets a text notification. No staff member had to trigger it. No phone call was needed. It just happens.

Auto-print removes another manual step. When a prescription is clinically cleared, the system moves it forward without someone needing to intervene. Work flows. The dispenser's job becomes dispensing, not chasing.
