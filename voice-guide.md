# Caption Voice Guide

The definitive reference for how Titan PMR and Titanverse captions should sound.
Built from analysis of 25+ top-performing golden era posts (Apr-Aug 2025).

Sound like a pharmacist talking to another pharmacist. Not a marketer. Not a SaaS company.

---

## The Golden Era Voice in One Sentence

Short, concrete, human. Real names, real numbers, real pharmacies. Storytelling, not explaining.

---

## Step Zero: Before Writing Anything

Answer one question in a single sentence:

> What is this caption *for*?

Not what it is about. What it is *for*.

If you can't answer that, the caption isn't ready to write.

---

## Sentence Structure

The rhythm is **short-short-short, then slightly longer, then back to short.**

Every paragraph = 1 sentence. Often a fragment. Reads like spoken word.

**Golden era example:**
> No overtime. No burnout.
> His pharmacy flows. His team stays calm.
> And it's not because he got lucky.
> It's because he built differently.

**Rules:**
- Sentences rarely exceed 12-15 words
- Line break after almost every sentence
- White space is a stylistic tool, the text breathes
- Long sentences earn their place by delivering the payoff or the data

---

## Opening Patterns

The first line is NEVER longer than ~10 words. The second line is often shorter. The first 10 words win or lose the caption.

### 1. Immediate Contrast
Before vs after. Problem vs solution. Old world vs new.
> 5 years ago, Prabjaudt was ready to leave pharmacy.

### 2. Bold Declarative
A short, punchy claim. No preamble.
> Pharmacy is broken.
> Titan checks every prescription. Every time.

### 3. The Flat Observation
State something true with no editorial. Let the reader feel the absurdity.
> The pharmacist had been clinical checking since 7am. It was 9pm.

### 4. The Named Person
Open with a real pharmacist. Named, specific, human.
> Rahul runs two branches and 18,000 items a month.

### 5. Specific Timeframe
Creates immediate story tension.
> 5 years ago. / Last September. / Three months into the new contract.

### 6. Direct Address ("you")
> Working in pharmacy? Then you've definitely heard one of these.
> If dispensing still eats every hour of your day...

### 7. Pithy/Playful (memes + culture posts)
> Pick your player.
> PAPER HAS BEEN ABDUCTED.

### What never works:
- "We're excited to announce..."
- "In today's pharmacy landscape..."
- "Hope this helps..."
- "Thoughts on this?"

## Caption Architectures

Choose one before writing. These describe how understanding unfolds for the reader.

**Chronological** — time as structure. Key developments in order. Works for transformation stories.

**Finish / Start / Finish** — open with the outcome, return to the beginning, work back. The reader knows where they're going so they stay for the journey. Works brilliantly for customer transformation posts.

**Zoom Out** — start hyper-specific. One pharmacy, one problem, one moment. Zoom out until the reader realises it's their problem too. Works for stance posts.

**Solving a Problem** — establish the problem, outline how it was addressed. Works for mechanism and product posts.

**What Someone Said** — anchor the entire caption on a quote. Build everything around it. Return to it. Works when the quote is genuinely striking.

**Block by Block** — each part only makes sense because of the part before it. Creates momentum and curiosity.

## Tone

Five registers, blended:

| Register | What it means |
|----------|--------------|
| **Confident, not arrogant** | States, doesn't hedge. Lets customer results do the bragging. |
| **Warm, not soft** | Care expressed through specificity (real names, real numbers), not sentimentality. |
| **Direct, not aggressive** | Blame aimed at systems, processes, tools. Never at people. |
| **Conversational, not casual** | Smart peer, not a sales pitch. Like someone just back from visiting a pharmacy. |
| **Playful, never cringe** | Dry, industry-specific humour. Never tries too hard. |

**Adjectives for the voice:** grounded, clear, human, empathetic, specific, purposeful, calm-but-urgent, peer-level.

## Vocabulary

### Always use:
| Word | Why |
|------|-----|
| **calm** | The north star word. Everything orbits the promise of calm. |
| **chaos** | The enemy. Always positioned as what Titan removes. |
| **flow / flows** | "His pharmacy flows." The aspiration. |
| **scale / scaling** | Growth without breaking. |
| **services** | The future. Dispensing is the past. |
| **bench** | Where pharmacists are trapped. "Stuck at the bench." |
| **time back** | The ultimate promise. Not "save time", "get time back." |
| **built** | "Built to scale." "Built differently." "Built for the future." |
| **paper** | The villain. Always the old way. |
| **baskets** | The physical symbol of dispensing pressure. |

### Never use:
| Word | Why |
|------|-----|
| revolutionary / game-changing | Corporate hype. Reads as AI. |
| best / leading / number one | No superlative self-praise. |
| cutting-edge / state-of-the-art | Tech jargon bravado. |
| excited / thrilled / delighted | Generic LinkedIn enthusiasm. |
| leverage / synergy / paradigm | Corporate buzzwords. |
| solution | Say what it DOES, not what it IS. |
| simply / just (dismissive) | Never trivialise pharmacy work. |
| incredible offer / limited time | Zero urgency-sales language. |
| streamline / empower | Vague SaaS buzzwords. Reads as AI. |
| testament / pivotal / landscape | Inflated meaning. Classic AI tells. |
| seamless / robust | Meaningless technical padding. |
| journey / ecosystem | Overused. Replace with a real noun. |

## Contractions: ALWAYS

The golden era uses contractions. The decline period stopped using them.

| Golden era | Decline period |
|-----------|---------------|
| it's | it is |
| can't | cannot |
| didn't | did not |
| that's | that is |
| wouldn't | would not |
| he's | he is |

**Use "does not" only for deliberate emphasis weight.** The phrase "does not" should feel like a hammer when it appears, reserved for moments where the formality IS the point.

## Emoji Rules

Emojis are **functional, not decorative.** They serve as bullet anchors or section markers.

**Per post:** 3-8 emojis. Never more than 10.

**Placement:**
- Opening lines: 0-1 emojis max. Often zero. Hook is text-only.
- Middle section (data/features): Emojis as bullet markers
- CTA section: 0-1 emojis

**Approved emojis:**
- Check marks: (most common, benefits/features)
- Package: (dispensing)
- Brain: (clinical/AI)
- Phone: (digital workflow)
- Clock: (time-saving)
- Book: (read the case study)
- Pointing: (swipe/read CTAs)
- Lightning: (speed/energy)
- Target/celebration: (milestones only)

**Never use:**
- Face emojis (no laughing, no heart-eyes, no clapping)
- Fire emoji
- Chains of 3+ emojis in a row
- Emojis in the hashtag section

---

## Punctuation

**No em dashes.** They are the most recognisable AI writing pattern. Use commas, full stops, or line breaks instead.

**Ellipses (...)** sparingly. Suspense or trailing into a reveal.
> From 4,000 items in an ex-Boots...

**Question marks** in openings and CTAs only.

**Exclamation marks** are RARE. Earned for genuine milestones only.

**Full stops** are the primary rhythmic tool. Each period = a beat of silence.

**No semicolons. No colons within sentences.** Colons only as list headers.

---

## Bullet/List Style

### Emoji-anchored bullets (features/benefits):
> ✅ Digital-first workflows
> ✅ AI-powered safety checks
> ✅ Real-time patient engagement

### Dash bullets (pain points, contrasts, lists):
> - Patient talks. You type.
> - You look at a screen. They look at the back of your head.
> - 10 minutes of admin after they leave.

**List items are short, one line each.** Never a paragraph inside a bullet.

## Retention Moves

Use when the caption needs them. Not as a checklist.

**Open Loop** — Raise a question early. Don't answer immediately. The gap pulls the reader forward.

**Progressive Disclosure** — Each line earns the next. The reader can't understand line four without lines one to three.

**Pattern Interrupt** — Break the rhythm deliberately. Three longer lines then one short one. The short line lands harder.

**The Pivot Word** — A single word that turns the caption. "But." "Fast forward." "Meanwhile."

**Direct Address** — "You" mid-caption pulls a drifting reader back in.

**Specificity as Anchor** — "20 hours a week" holds attention longer than "significant time savings."

**The Callback** — Return to the opening image or phrase at the close. Creates completion.

**White Space** — A single line as its own paragraph signals: this matters.

---

## Quote Handling

**The Emotional Climax (customer voice)** — Numbers first. Quote second. Meaning third. The quote lands harder because the evidence has already done its work.
> 20 pharmacist hours a week saved. "I'm not glued to the bench. We're now a community health hub." Rahul's story isn't just about efficiency.

**The Hook (leadership voice only)** — Quote first. Attribution. Then context. Only works when the quote is genuinely striking and from someone with authority.

**The Stance Delivery (industry data)** — Setup first. Controversial stat mid-caption. Consequences after.

**What never works:** Opening with a customer quote. Closing on a quote without adding meaning. Anonymous testimonials.

## CTA Patterns

### 1. Aspiration Question (highest engagement):
> What would you do with 80% of your time back?
> Could your pharmacy work without you?

### 2. Industry Dialogue:
> What needs to change before October?

### 3. Value Continuation:
> 📖 Read the full case study: [link]

### 4. Confident Statement (no ask needed):
> No paper. No panic. No wasted time. This is The Titan Way.

### 5. Utility Action:
> Save this. Share it. Talk to your team.

### 6. The Soft Nudge:
> titanpmr.com/join-us

**The final line is NEVER a sales pitch.** Always a question, a human acknowledgment, or a punchy closer.

**What never works:** "Find this helpful? Like & repost." / "What do you think?" / "Tag someone who needs this." / Four asks at once. / Generic questions with no real interest behind them.

## Hashtags

**Count:** 5-8 per post. Up to 10 for reactive/industry posts with topic-specific tags.

Golden era data: average 6.4, mode 5, top 10 performers averaged 7.4.

**Placement:** Always at the very end. Separated by a line break. An afterthought, not part of the narrative.

**Standard set:** #TitanPMR, #CommunityPharmacy, #DigitalPharmacy, #PharmacyWorkflow, #PharmacyFirst

**Titanverse set:** #Titanverse, #PharmacyServices, #AIinPharmacy, #DigitalHealth

**Never:** Hashtags mid-sentence. Hashtags in the opening. Sentence-case hashtags.

## Perspective

| Post type | Perspective | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Case study | Third person | "Sagar didn't just buy a pharmacy. He rebuilt it." |
| Industry/opinion | First person plural ("we") | "We've spoken to pharmacists across the country." |
| Direct-to-audience | Second person ("you") | "If you want your pharmacy to grow..." |
| Customer quotes | Attributed directly | "I'm not glued to the bench." Rahul. |

**The brand NEVER speaks in first person singular ("I").** Always "we" or the company name.

## Signature Moves

### 1. The "No X. No Y. No Z." triple negation
> No chaos. No compromise.
> No overtime. No burnout.
> No paper. No panic. No wasted time.

### 2. The reframe
The emotional pivot from what something isn't to what it is. Avoid the literal "Not X, but Y" structure (reads as AI). Instead, make the contrast land through juxtaposition, line breaks, or storytelling.

**Avoid:**
> Not about checking less. About checking smarter.

**Better:**
> Everyone talks about checking less. The real shift is checking smarter.

> They thought they needed more staff. They needed a better system.

> The goal was never fewer checks. It was smarter ones.

The contrast still lands. The construction feels human.

### 3. The transformation one-liner
> From 4,000 to 17,000 items.
> 20+ hours saved per pharmacist.
> 80% of his time on services.

### 4. The "That's what changed" pivot
> That's what Titan AI delivers.
> That's what changed in Sagar's pharmacy.

### 5. The "In his/her own words" quote intro
> In his own words:
> Amanda put it simply:

### 6. The "But here's the truth:" pivot
> It sounds efficient. But it isn't safe.
> But here's the truth: Clinical pharmacy needs more than admin support.

### 7. The "Just..." simplicity payoff
> Just a calm, efficient system built right from the start.
> Just calm, clinical pharmacy at scale.

### 8. The "This is what X looks like" declaration
> This is what a modern pharmacy looks like.
> This is pharmacy with backup. Not burnout.

## The Physical Wince Test

If reading it back makes you uncomfortable, the reader will feel it too. Trust the reflex.

**Mid-caption moves that fail:**
- The Apologetic Qualifier: "Now, we know this might not work for everyone, but..." You just undermined your own authority.
- The Feature List Dump: a checklist of features with no transformation story.
- The Fake Transition: "But here's the thing..." / "Here's what's interesting..." Manufactured drama.
- The Humble Brag: false modesty while flexing numbers. Pick one.

**Closes that fail:**
- The Desperate Triple-Ask: liking, reposting, commenting, following, all at once.
- The Fake Curiosity Close: "Would love to hear your take." Nobody believes this.
- The Manufactured Urgency: "Don't miss out on this game-changing opportunity!"
- The Self-Congratulatory Sign-Off: making it about your feelings instead of their outcomes.

---

## The Authority Test

Read the caption back. Would a successful pharmacy owner say this to a colleague?

If no, rewrite.

---

## What Killed the Golden Era (voice edition)

| Golden era | Decline |
|-----------|---------|
| Storytelling | Explaining |
| People (Sagar, Rahul, Prab) | Archetypes ("most pharmacies") |
| Concrete ("baskets piling up") | Abstract ("when workflow is clear, pressure doesn't escalate") |
| Contractions ("it's", "can't") | Formal ("it is", "cannot") |
| Short captions, trust the format | Long essays, over-explain |
| Earned emotion from specificity | Stated emotion from assertions |
| Dispatches from real pharmacies | Thought leadership essays |

## Pre-Publish Voice Check

Before posting any caption, verify:

- [ ] First line is under 10 words
- [ ] Uses contractions throughout (it's, can't, didn't, that's)
- [ ] At least one real name or specific number appears
- [ ] No banned vocabulary (solution, revolutionary, excited, leverage, streamline, seamless)
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] No "Not X, but Y" literal pattern
- [ ] Emojis are functional (bullet markers), not decorative
- [ ] CTA is a question or soft nudge, not a sales pitch
- [ ] Reads like conversation, not a white paper
- [ ] Has at least one signature move
- [ ] Could be read aloud without sounding corporate
- [ ] "Calm" or its emotional equivalent is somewhere in the DNA of the post
- [ ] Passes the authority test: would a pharmacy owner say this to a colleague?
- [ ] Passes the wince test: nothing makes you cringe reading it back
- [ ] 5-8 hashtags at the end, separated by a line break
