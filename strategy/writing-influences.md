# Writing Influences

## Three frameworks. One combined model.

These are not style guides to mimic.  
They are thinking tools to apply before and during writing.

---

## Ros Atkins — The Art of Explanation

**The structural framework.**

Atkins is the BBC journalist behind the viral "Ros Atkins on..." explainer videos.  
His core argument: most communication fails before a word is written, because the purpose isn't clear enough yet.

---

### Before you write anything

Answer one question in a single sentence:

> What is this post *for*?

Not what it is about. What it is *for*.

If you cannot answer that, the post is not ready.

---

### The two-sweep distillation

Gather everything you know about the topic.  
Then cut twice before writing.

**First sweep** — remove anything irrelevant to the purpose.

**Second sweep** — remove anything that isn't actively serving the one thing the post needs to do.

What remains is the post.

---

### The 10 attributes (editing checklist)

Most useful for Titan content:

**Simplicity** — clarity of language, not necessarily brevity. Remove distractions.

**Essential detail** — every non-essential piece makes the essential harder to land.

**Context** — have you said why this matters to a pharmacy owner, not just that it exists?

**Useful** — are you answering a question they are already walking around with?

**No distractions** — any language that needs translating should go.

**Clarity of purpose** — above all else, what are you trying to explain?

---

### The 7-step process (for longer pieces)

1. Set-up — define purpose, audience, specific questions to answer
2. Gather — more raw material than you need
3. Distil — two sweeps, ruthless
4. Organise — sections in logical order, high-impact material flagged for open and close
5. Link — write the first draft from the structure
6. Review — does it do what step 1 said it should?
7. Edit — precision and redundancy

## Mehdi Hasan — Win Every Argument

**The persuasion framework.**

Hasan draws on Aristotle's three modes: ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), logos (logic).  
His central argument: pathos almost always lands before logos. People feel their way to a position, then rationalise it with facts.

---

### Sequence matters

**Emotion first. Logic second.**

The Titan posts that work open with a recognisable emotional state.  
The pharmacist still at the bench at 8pm.  
The consultation that fell through the cracks.  
The audit that exposed a governance gap.

That is pathos. The product or mechanism comes after.  
Lead with the feature and you lose them before the story lands.

---

### The rule of three

Working memory holds roughly three chunks at once.  
Three points. Three words. Three slides.

Beyond three, readers start losing the thread.  
Apply deliberately to: hooks, carousel key points, caption structure.

---

### Rhetorical judo

Do not lead with a frontal argument.  
Name the objection before the reader forms it.

> "Most pharmacy software companies will tell you their system is different. Here's what different actually looks like."

You have taken their counter-argument and used it as your opening.  
That is pre-emption. It is disarming and builds credibility.

---

### Ethos: earn the right to be believed

Credibility comes before the argument.  
In content terms: proof before claim.  
Customer voice before product statement.  
Demonstrated change before promised outcome.

---

## Romesh Ranganathan — Straight Outta Crawley

**The voice framework.**

Ranganathan's comedy is deadpan, self-deprecating, and structured around deliberate tangents.  
His memoir is subtitled *Memoirs of a Distinctly Average Human Being*.  
That rhetorical position — honest, unglamorous, specific — is the whole game.

---

### The aside

The parenthetical that does all the personality work while the main sentence stays functional.

In caption writing this is the difference between a post that is correct and a post that sounds like a person.

Write the functional sentence.  
Then find the aside that reframes it quietly.

---

### The deadpan gap

The comedy in his writing lives in the distance between the flat delivery and the absurdity of what is being described.

For Titan: name something frustrating with complete composure.  
No editorial outrage. No "this is unacceptable."  
Just the observation, delivered straight.

> "The pharmacist had been clinical checking since 7am. It was 9pm."

Let the reader feel the absurdity themselves.  
The flat delivery is what makes it land.

---

### The deliberate tangent

State the thing.  
Wander somewhere adjacent that makes the reader feel something.  
Come back and land the original point — now heavier because of where you have been.

Not a digression. A detour with a purpose.  
Useful in longer captions and video scripts.

---

### Foolish honesty

His own description of his approach: honesty at what some would describe as a foolish level.

Not performing relatability. Actually exposing the gap between how things are supposed to go and how they actually went.

In pharmacy terms: a pharmacist saying "the system still isn't good enough, here's exactly how bad it is" is more compelling than one enthusiastically selling a product.  
Misanthropy deployed precisely and honestly reads as warmth.

---

## The combined model

| Problem | Framework | Tool |
|---------|-----------|------|
| Unclear structure | Atkins | One-sentence purpose. Two-sweep distillation. |
| Wrong sequence | Hasan | Emotion before logic. Pre-empt the objection. |
| Sounds produced | Ranganathan | The aside. The deadpan gap. Foolish honesty. |

Most pharmacy tech content hits none of the three.  
It is structurally unclear, leads with features, and sounds like a press release.

That gap is the opportunity.
