# Motion Design Guide

## The Standard

A good motion design video is clear communication unfolding over time.

Not: does it look good?  
Ask: does it make the viewer understand, feel, and remember something?

---

## What Motion Does

**Clarify** — simplifies systems, sequences, comparisons, invisible processes.  
**Direct attention** — movement tells the eye where to look. Move the right thing at the right time.  
**Control sequence** — you decide what appears first, what stays hidden, when the key point lands.  
**Add emotional shape** — the way something moves carries tone: confident, calm, urgent, premium.  
**Make ideas memorable** — contrast, reveals, and process give the brain something to hold onto.

---

## The Four Layers

Every strong piece needs all four:

**1. Idea** — What is the actual point? One sentence. If you can't say it in one sentence, the project isn't ready.

**2. Structure** — What order does information arrive in?  
Good structures: problem → solution → outcome / before → breakthrough → after / tension → reveal → clarity

**3. Design** — Layout, typography, colour, composition. Makes the piece feel coherent and intentional.

**4. Motion** — Timing, easing, rhythm, restraint. Supports the idea. Never overpowers it.

---

## Storytelling Techniques

**Contrast** — the strongest tool. Before/after. Chaos/order. Manual/automatic. Without contrast, motion feels empty.

**Progressive disclosure** — don't reveal everything at once. Orient → problem → mechanism → proof → outcome.

**Curiosity gaps** — open with a question before answering it. If there's no question, there's no reason to continue.

**The reveal** — when confusion turns into clarity. The most satisfying moment motion can create. Make the viewer feel insight, not just see it.

**Emotional arc** — curiosity → friction → understanding → relief → confidence. Even explainers need emotional shape.

**Human anchoring** — a voiceover, quote, face, or recognisable problem. Pure abstraction goes cold.

**Proof** — data, examples, real quotes, demonstrated change. Without it, motion is visual theatre.

---

## Key Principles

**Clarity** — the viewer always knows what they're looking at, what matters, what changed.

**Hierarchy** — one moment, one dominant idea. The eye should be led.

**Rhythm** — know when to move, pause, breathe, hold. Stillness can be powerful. Confidence, not panic.

**Restraint** — not everything needs to animate. Movement is most powerful when selective.

**Specificity** — if the visuals could belong to any company, they're too vague. Abstract from something real.

**Cohesion** — shape language, typography, colour, pacing, tone. One world.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- No clear point — style without idea
- Wrong structure — information in the wrong order
- Too much happening — everything competes, nothing leads
- No contrast — never shows what changed or why it matters
- Over-animation — movement becomes noise
- No emotional arc — explains but doesn't move
- No proof — claims feel airy
- No breathing room — viewer never gets to process anything

---

## When to Use Motion

**Use it when:**
- Something changes over time
- A process or system needs explaining
- A comparison needs to be made clear
- Information would be too dense as a still

**Don't use it when:**
- A still or quote would be stronger
- The main value is human authenticity
- The idea is already instantly obvious

---

## Framework for Any Motion Piece

1. Define the core idea — one sentence
2. Choose the story structure — how does understanding unfold?
3. Identify the key contrast — what is changing?
4. Decide what deserves motion — what genuinely benefits from animation?
5. Build hierarchy scene by scene — what does the viewer notice first?
6. Create rhythm — where does it move, pause, simplify, land?
7. Ground it — what makes it feel real or emotionally anchored?
8. Strip the unnecessary — good motion design is often about removing distraction

---

## Review Questions

- What is the actual point?
- What question is the viewer asking at the start?
- What is the strongest contrast?
- Where is the eye meant to go?
- Is the motion clarifying or decorating?
- Does the piece breathe?
- What will the viewer still understand 10 minutes later?

---

*The goal is not to make something move. It's to make meaning unfold well over time.*
