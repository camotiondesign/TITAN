# Anti-AI Writing Guide
*Based on Wikipedia's "Signs of AI Writing" — patterns observed across thousands of AI-generated texts.*

---

## How to Use This File

Read before writing anything.

These are patterns to avoid, not rules. The goal is not to hide that AI helped — it is to write clearly, specifically, and like a person with real knowledge of the subject.

The most AI-sounding writing fails not because of individual phrases. It fails because the thinking underneath is hollow. Fix the thinking first. The words follow.

---

## 1. Inflated Symbolism and Meaning

AI inflates the importance of things by connecting them to vague broader themes.

Cut or rewrite anything containing:

- stands as a testament
- plays a vital / significant role
- underscores its importance
- continue to captivate
- leaves a lasting impact
- watershed moment
- key turning point
- deeply rooted
- profound heritage
- steadfast dedication
- solidifies
- pivotal moment
- marking a new chapter

**Why it happens:** AI is trained to make content feel meaningful. It adds significance that the facts don't earn on their own.

**Fix:** State what actually happened. Let the reader decide if it matters.

---

## 2. Promotional Tone

AI defaults to the language of tourism and marketing, even in neutral contexts.

Watch for:

- rich cultural heritage
- rich history
- breathtaking
- must-visit / must-see
- stunning natural beauty
- enduring legacy
- lasting legacy
- rich cultural tapestry
- vibrant community

**Fix:** Describe specifically. "Bustling" is meaningless. Say what is actually happening.

---

## 3. Editorialising

AI adds interpretation or opinion even when none was asked for. It also announces what it is about to do, or reminds you that you are reading a piece of content.

Cut phrases like:

- it's important to note / remember / consider
- it is worth noting
- no discussion would be complete without
- in this article
- as we explore
- needless to say

**Fix:** Trust the reader. If something matters, show why — don't announce that it matters.

---

## 4. Overused Connective Phrases

AI relies on a small set of transitions to imply depth and flow. These phrases are not wrong in isolation. The problem is the pattern — they appear constantly, implying analysis that is not there.

Overused:

- moreover
- furthermore
- additionally
- in addition
- on the other hand
- in contrast
- it is worth noting
- to this end

**Fix:** Cut most of them. If the logic is clear, you do not need a signpost for it.

---

## 5. Negative Parallelism ("Not X, but Y")

This is the single most identifiable AI writing pattern.

AI loves the structure: "It is not just X — it is Y."

Examples:

- "It's not just about the beat; it's part of the atmosphere."
- "Mobile isn't just first — it's everything."
- "Not just a platform, but a movement."

It also appears across two sentences:

> "He came from a distinguished family. His life, however, took a different path."

**Fix:** Make the point directly. If Y is the point, say Y. You do not need X as a runway.

---

## 6. Superficial Analysis — The Trailing "-ing" Problem

AI adds hollow analysis at the end of sentences using present participles. These words gesture at meaning without delivering it.

Watch for sentences ending in:

- ensuring...
- highlighting...
- emphasising...
- reflecting...
- demonstrating...
- underscoring...
- illustrating...

Example:

> "Customers benefit from faster service, improving convenience."

**Fix:** Either make the point substantive or cut the tail entirely. "Customers get faster service" is stronger.

---

## 7. Vague Attribution ("Weasel Wording")

AI attributes claims to unnamed authorities to create the impression of evidence.

Phrases to cut:

- industry reports suggest
- observers have noted
- experts believe
- many have argued
- some critics say
- it has been widely noted

**Fix:** Name the source or cut the claim. Vague attribution is usually a sign the claim cannot be verified.

---

## 8. Bullet Points with Bold Titles

A distinctively AI structure: bolded label followed by a sentence that simply restates the label.

Example:

> **Scalability:** The system is designed to scale easily across different use cases.

The bold phrase adds no information. The sentence just repeats it.

**Fix:** Write in prose, or use bullets only when there is genuine list-like content. If you must use bullets, remove the bolded label — it is almost always redundant.

---

## 9. Em Dashes

AI uses em dashes far more than human writers do, and in places where a comma, parenthesis, or colon would be more natural.

The tell is not using em dashes — it is using them to punch up ordinary clauses in a sales-like way.

**Fix:** Replace with commas or parentheses. If you cannot decide which, use a comma.

---

## 10. Uniform Sentence Length and Structure

AI produces writing where every sentence is roughly the same length. Every paragraph is the same size. The rhythm becomes predictable and lifeless.

**Fix:** Vary pacing deliberately. Short sentences when something matters. Longer sentences when you are building to something. A single short line can be a paragraph.

---

## 11. False Profundity — The Meaning-Making Machine

AI is trained to sound meaningful. It wraps ordinary information in the language of significance.

This produces writing that feels like it is always on the verge of saying something important, but never quite does.

Signs:

- Sentences that sound quotable but say nothing specific
- Endings that restate the beginning as if it were a revelation
- Generic conclusions about "the future of" any given subject

**Fix:** Earn the weight. If you are making a claim about significance, back it with something real.

---

## 12. The "Not Just" Opener

A specific variant of negative parallelism. AI loves to open with a dismissal of a smaller thing before introducing the real point.

> "This is not just a tool — it is a transformation."
> "It is not simply about convenience."
> "Pharmacy is not just about dispensing."

**Fix:** Start with the real point.

---

## 13. Announcing Caveats Before Making Points

AI often hedges before it commits to anything.

> "While it is true that... it is also important to recognise..."
> "Although many factors are at play, one key consideration is..."

**Fix:** Make the point. If a caveat is genuinely necessary, put it after.

## The Underlying Problem

AI writing sounds like AI writing because the thinking is generic.

It has no opinion. It has no specific knowledge. It has no experience of the subject. So it covers the gap with intensity — bigger words, more transitions, more signposting — to create the impression of depth.

The fix is not better prompts. It is specificity.

A specific fact is more convincing than a well-structured paragraph about nothing.

A real quote beats a manufactured insight every time.

An honest sentence about what actually happened is better than three sentences about why it matters.

Write less. Observe more. Know the subject.
